Coral Reefs

Coral reefs are the rainforests of the sea, home to 25% of all marine life. They offer countless benefits to our ecosystems and are an essential part of coastal protection and biodiversity.

Coral reefs in India are one of the most ancient and dynamic ecosystems. India has about 7517 km of coastline including islands, with the mainland coast at 6100 km. These reefs are mainly in the Andaman and Nicobar Islands, Gulf of Mannar, Gulf of Kutch, Palk Strait, and the Lakshadweep islands. All are fringing reefs except Lakshadweep which are atolls. Patchy corals are also found along the intertidal areas of the central west coast like Ratnagiri, Gaveshani Bank, etc.

Hermatypic corals are present along the sea shore from Kollam in Kerala to Enayam Puthenthurai in Tamil Nadu.

How are they formed?

Coral polyps are small, fleshy sea anemones found in tropical waters. They live in a symbiotic relationship with microscopic algae (Zooxanthellae). The algae perform photosynthesis and feed the polyps; the polyps protect the algae.
Coral reefs form when coral larvae settle on hard surfaces near coastlines, aided by sedimentation, compaction, and cementation of coral skeletons.

Advantages of Coral Reefs

  • Marine Biodiversity: Shelter and food for marine species.
  • Coastal Protection: Barrier from waves and storms.
  • Tourism and Economy: Drives economic growth.
  • Medicinal Uses: Marine organisms for medical research.

Threats to Coral Reefs

  • Climate Change: Coral bleaching due to rising temperatures.
  • Overfishing: Disrupts coral ecosystems.
